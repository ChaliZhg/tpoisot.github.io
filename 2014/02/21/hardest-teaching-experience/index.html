My worst experience as a teacher

21 Feb 2014

The other day, I was part of a short discussion about our worst teaching experiences. I was wondering between the time a first-year student set its bench and lab report on fire (albeit for a short time, for ethanol is a good combustible), and the time a second year student told me I should cut the class on classical population dynamics models, because what is the point of that for ecologists? Then a few days later, I remembered another incident, which was much less likely to make people laugh; but also one that was much more difficult to handle.

I was a moniteur during my PhD. Being a moniteur is much like being a teacher assistant, with almost no supervision, absolutely no formal training (though we did have an introductory class on self expression, and I have a vague recollection of failing at my Basic computer litteracy test, which ought to be called Using Microsoft Word and PowerPoint). Your only duty as a moniteur is to fill your quota of hours, 64 hours a year. But because you cannot do less than 64 hours, and if classes come in 20 or 40 hours, unless you can find someone to give you very short teaching gigs to reach exactly 64 hours, you’re in for (unpaid, did I need to mention) overtime. And for some reason, spending one actual hour in front of a class may count for 30, 60, or 90 minutes of work, so if you are unlucky, or if your teaching mentor has no nice gigs to give you, you can expect to do over 100 hours of effective teaching each year (not counting preparation, grading, and exam

But such is one (out of many) cost that comes with having a great university system: one that allows anyone to enter regardless of income.

It would be tempting to assume that Montpellier (the city, and the university), is a hotbed of soial mixity. As in almost all old cities in France, there is a still present old bourgeoisie, and a new working and lower class peole making (approximately) 80% of the overall population. All of these people hang out in (almost) the same streets, and go to (almost) the same universities. But that is far from the truth; Montpellier is a great example of social juxtaposition, where people from different classes co-occur but seldom interact.

The fascinating thing is that, especially in the school system, some try to fake it. It’s fashionable to adopt dressing codes and attitudes (or perceptions of the attitudes) of “the others”. Dressing though is a way not to be ostracized for the fact that you have more disposable income than 80% of your classmates, and dressing nice (loosely meaning, expensive), is a way not to project that you are in the lower 10%.
